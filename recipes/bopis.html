Bopis

History

Bopis is a bold and flavorful Filipino dish made primarily from finely chopped pork offal—traditionally lungs and heart—sautéed with onions, garlic, ginger, chilies, and vinegar. Its origins trace back to the Spanish colonial period, when Spanish settlers introduced offal-based cooking techniques and dishes such as callos. Over time, Filipinos adapted these influences using local ingredients and tastes, creating a uniquely Filipino preparation marked by sharp acidity, heat, and aromatics.

Historically, bopis emerged as a practical and communal dish, reflecting the Filipino value of minimizing waste by using every part of the animal. It becline breakame especially popular as pulutan—food eaten with alcohol—because of its spicy, tangy profile that pairs well with beer or spirits. Today, bopis remains a staple in carinderias and celebrations, symbolizing Filipino resourcefulness, culinary adaptability, and the enduring blend of indigenous and colonial influences in Philippine cuisine.

Directions

  1. Prep meat: Boil pork lungs/heart (and liver if using) until tender. Drain, cool, then chop finely.
  2. Sauté aromatics: Heat oil. Sauté onion, garlic, and ginger until fragrant.
  3. Add meat: Stir in chopped offal. Cook 3 to 5 minutes.
  4. Season: Add bay leaves, salt, pepper, and soy sauce (optional). Mix well.
  5. Add vinegar + heat: Pour in vinegar (don’t stir for 1 minute), then stir and simmer 5 to 10 minutes. Add a splash of water/broth if it’s too dry.
  6. Make it spicy : Add chilies and cook 1 to 2 more minutes.
  7. Finish: Taste and adjust—more vinegar, salt, or pepper. Optional: add calamansi at the end. Serve hot.
